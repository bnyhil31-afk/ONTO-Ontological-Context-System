SCOPE AND PURPOSE

This document describes ONTO's compliance architecture — how the system's
design satisfies regulatory requirements across privacy, data protection,
safe messaging, and content classification.

It is written for legal counsel, compliance reviewers, and deployers.
It is not a substitute for legal advice. Deployments in regulated domains
(healthcare, finance, legal, education) must obtain independent legal
review before going live.

Items marked [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED] are flagged in CRE-SPEC-001 §33
and must be reviewed by qualified counsel before public launch.

ITEM 5.01 — GDPR ARCHITECTURE
Right to Erasure vs Append-Only Audit Trail

REGULATION: GDPR Article 17 (Right to Erasure / Right to be Forgotten)

THE TENSION:
  GDPR Article 17 grants individuals the right to have their personal
  data erased. ONTO's audit trail is append-only by design — records
  cannot be deleted or modified. These appear to conflict.

RESOLUTION — CRYPTOGRAPHIC ERASURE:
  The two-layer record structure resolves this conflict without
  compromising either principle.

  Layer 1 — Shell (metadata):
    Stored in the `events` table. Contains event type, timestamp,
    classification level, chain_hash. Contains NO personal data.
    This layer persists permanently.

  Layer 2 — Payload (personal data):
    Currently stored in the `notes`, `input`, `context`, `output`,
    and `human_decision` fields within the events table.

  CRYPTOGRAPHIC ERASURE PROCESS:
    When a right-to-erasure request is received:
    1. The encryption key for the relevant data is destroyed
    2. The encrypted payload becomes permanently unreadable
    3. The shell record persists — the audit trail remains intact
    4. The chain is not broken — integrity is preserved

  LEGAL BASIS:
    The Article 29 Working Party and the European Data Protection Board
    have recognized cryptographic erasure as compliant deletion under
    GDPR when:
      (a) the data was encrypted before storage
      (b) the key is genuinely destroyed (not just inaccessible)
      (c) the encrypted data cannot practically be decrypted

  CURRENT STATUS:
    Stage 1 implements application-layer AES-256-GCM encryption
    (core/encryption.py). The key infrastructure for erasure-by-
    key-destruction exists but the erasure workflow is not yet
    implemented as a user-facing feature. [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]

  STAGE 1 GUIDANCE:
    Do not store sensitive personal data in a Stage 1 deployment until
    the erasure workflow is implemented and reviewed. Stage 1 is
    appropriate for local single-user deployment with data the operator
    controls entirely.

ITEM 5.02 — CCPA COMPLIANCE REVIEW
California Consumer Privacy Act

REGULATION: CCPA / CPRA (California Civil Code §1798.100 et seq.)

APPLICABILITY:
  CCPA applies to for-profit businesses meeting threshold criteria
  (revenue, data volume, proportion of revenue from data sales).
  ONTO as currently deployed (open source, non-commercial,
  single-user) likely does not meet CCPA thresholds.
  [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED for commercial deployments]

RIGHTS MAPPING:

  Right to Know (§1798.100):
    ONTO provides complete transparency through the audit trail.
    Every piece of data the system has processed is recorded
    permanently and readable via `memory.read_all()` or `GET /audit`.
    This exceeds the right-to-know requirement.

  Right to Delete (§1798.105):
    Addressed via the cryptographic erasure architecture described
    in item 5.01. Operator-controlled: the operator can destroy the
    encryption key, rendering all data permanently unreadable.
    [Implementation: complete when erasure workflow ships]

  Right to Opt-Out of Sale (§1798.120):
    ONTO does not sell, share, or transfer personal data. The system
    is local-first by design. No data leaves the device. There is
    nothing to opt out of.

  Right to Non-Discrimination (§1798.125):
    Not applicable to the current deployment model.

  Right to Correct (§1798.106 — CPRA addition):
    ONTO's append-only audit trail does not support correction of
    individual records. New records can be written that supersede
    or annotate earlier records. A formal correction workflow is
    a Stage 2+ item. [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED]

ITEM 5.03 — DATA RETENTION POLICY

PURPOSE:
  Defines how long ONTO retains processed data, why, and what happens
  when data is no longer needed.

AUDIT TRAIL (modules/memory.py):
  The audit trail is permanent by design. Records are never
  automatically deleted. This is a core architectural commitment:
  the audit trail is the system's immune memory. Every decision
  made with ONTO is recorded permanently so it can be reviewed,
  audited, and learned from.

  Retention period: Indefinite (append-only, no expiry)
  Rationale: Audit integrity, accountability, forensic capability
  Deletion: Operator-initiated cryptographic erasure only

SESSION DATA (core/session.py):
  Session records exist only in memory. They are never written to
  disk except for SESSION_START and SESSION_END events in the audit
  trail (without the raw token — only the token_prefix).
  Sessions expire automatically:
    - Idle timeout: 1 hour (configurable via ONTO_SESSION_TTL_SECONDS)
    - Hard maximum: 8 hours (configurable via ONTO_SESSION_MAX_LIFETIME_SECONDS)
  After expiry or explicit logout, the session record is removed
  from memory immediately (GDPR Article 5(1)(c): data minimisation).

CONTEXTUAL FIELD (modules/contextualize.py):
  The living field — the set of past inputs used for contextualisation
  — is stored in memory and persisted to the audit trail. It is
  rebuilt from memory on each restart. Entries in the field decay
  in weight over time (contextual decay) but are not automatically
  deleted from the audit trail.

CONFIGURATION:
  No automatic retention limits are currently implemented. A deployer
  who needs retention limits should implement a scheduled erasure
  job that destroys the encryption key for data older than the
  retention period. [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED for regulated deployments]

EU AI ACT (Article 26):
  High-risk deployments must retain logs for a minimum of 6 months.
  ONTO's indefinite retention satisfies this requirement. Deployers
  in high-risk domains must ensure the key is not destroyed within
  6 months of the relevant events.

ITEM 5.04 — CONSENT MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORK

CURRENT STATE (Stage 1):
  In Stage 1 single-user local deployment, the operator IS the user.
  Implicit consent exists by virtue of the operator deploying and
  using their own system. No separate consent collection is required.

  The session schema includes a `consent_reference` field (currently
  None in Stage 1) that is designed to carry a pointer to a consent
  ledger record in Stage 2+. This forward-compatibility is already
  implemented in core/session.py.

STAGE 2 CONSENT ARCHITECTURE:
  When ONTO is deployed as a multi-user system, the consent management
  framework will implement:

  1. CONSENT COLLECTION
     Every new user session requires an active consent record.
     Consent is specific (stated purpose), informed (plain language
     description of what data is processed), freely given (no coercion),
     and unambiguous (explicit action required).

  2. CONSENT LEDGER
     Consent records are stored permanently in the audit trail with:
       - Consenting party identity
       - Purpose scope
       - Data categories consented to
       - Timestamp and expiry
       - Revocation timestamp (if revoked)
     The ledger is append-only — revocation is recorded as a new
     record, not by modifying the original consent record.

  3. CONSENT VERIFICATION
     Every processing action checks for a valid, non-expired consent
     record before proceeding. Expired or revoked consent causes the
     system to return to the consent collection flow.

  4. CONSENT REVOCATION
     Users can revoke consent at any time. Revocation is immediate —
     no further processing occurs under the revoked consent. The
     revocation event is recorded permanently.

  5. W3C VERIFIABLE CREDENTIALS (Stage 3+)
     Consent records are designed to be forward-compatible with
     W3C VC 2.0 format (W3C Recommendation, May 2025). The fields
     are the same. Migration to signed, portable VCs requires no
     schema change. See CRE-SPEC-001 §16 for the ledger schema.

GDPR ARTICLES ADDRESSED:
  Article 6: Lawful basis — consent or legitimate interest
  Article 7: Conditions for consent — specific, informed, unambiguous
  Article 13/14: Transparency — clear purpose in consent presentation
  Article 17: Right to erasure — consent revocation + cryptographic erasure

ITEM 5.06 — COOKIE AND TRACKING POLICY

COOKIES:
  ONTO does not use cookies. The HTTP API uses bearer tokens
  (Authorization header), not cookies. There is no session cookie,
  no tracking cookie, no analytics cookie.

TRACKING:
  ONTO collects no telemetry, analytics, or usage data of any kind.
  No data leaves the device. The system has no outbound network
  connections during normal operation.

THIRD-PARTY SERVICES:
  None. ONTO has no third-party integrations, SDKs, or external
  service calls in its processing pipeline.

BROWSER STORAGE:
  The API server does not set any browser storage (localStorage,
  sessionStorage, IndexedDB). The /docs and /redoc endpoints
  load Swagger UI and ReDoc from their bundled assets — no CDN,
  no external scripts.

EPERIVACY DIRECTIVE / COOKIE LAW:
  The cookie law does not apply to ONTO because ONTO uses no cookies
  or similar tracking technologies. A cookie banner is not required.

IF A WEB FRONTEND IS ADDED:
  Any web frontend added to ONTO must:
  1. Not use third-party analytics scripts
  2. Not set tracking cookies
  3. Use bearer tokens (not cookies) for session management
  4. Display a privacy notice if personal data is collected via forms
  Update this policy when a frontend is added.

ITEM 5.07 — DATA CLASSIFICATION AT INTAKE

OVERVIEW:
  Every input to ONTO is assigned a classification level (0-5) by
  the intake module (modules/intake.py, item C3 from REVIEW_001).
  This level propagates through all downstream modules and is stored
  in every audit record.

CLASSIFICATION LEVELS:

  Level 0 — UNCLASSIFIED
    General text with no personal or sensitive elements.
    Examples: "what is the weather today", "explain recursion"
    Retention: Indefinite audit trail
    Encryption: Recommended but not required

  Level 1 — INTERNAL
    Content that is sensitive in context but not personally identifying.
    Examples: internal business questions, proprietary processes
    Retention: Indefinite audit trail
    Encryption: Recommended

  Level 2 — PERSONAL (GDPR "personal data")
    Data that identifies or could identify an individual.
    Triggered by: name references, location, contact details,
    date of birth, relationship references.
    GDPR Article 4(1): "personal data means any information relating
    to an identified or identifiable natural person"
    Retention: Indefinite audit trail; right to erasure applies
    Encryption: Required for production deployments
    Read logging: Enabled (READ_ACCESS events generated)

  Level 3 — SENSITIVE (GDPR "special categories")
    Triggered by: health/medical terms, financial data, legal data,
    biometric references, authentication credentials.
    GDPR Article 9: Special categories of personal data.
    HIPAA: Protected Health Information (PHI) if applicable.
    Retention: Indefinite; right to erasure applies; HIPAA 6-year minimum
    Encryption: Required
    Read logging: Enabled
    Access control: Checkpoint always required (Stage 2+)

  Level 4 — RESTRICTED
    Reserved for future use. For classified deployments where
    information controls apply.

  Level 5 — HIGHEST SENSITIVITY
    Reserved for future use. For deployments requiring the highest
    possible access controls.

REGULATORY MAPPING:

  Classification → GDPR category:
    0-1: Non-personal data (no GDPR basis required)
    2:   Personal data (GDPR Article 6 lawful basis required)
    3:   Special categories (GDPR Article 9 explicit consent required)
    4-5: Operator-defined restricted categories

  Classification → HIPAA:
    0-2: Not PHI (unless context makes it so)
    3+:  Treat as PHI in healthcare deployments; apply BAA requirements

  Classification → CCPA:
    0-1: Non-personal information
    2-3: Personal information (CCPA rights apply for covered businesses)

IMPLEMENTATION:
  The classification field is computed in modules/intake.py using
  keyword pattern matching. Current implementation detects levels 0-3.
  Levels 4-5 are reserved for operator-defined patterns.

  Future improvement: ML-based classification for higher accuracy,
  replacing keyword matching (REVIEW_001 Exploration E5).

ITEM 5.08 — SAFE MESSAGING COMPLIANCE

REGULATIONS AND GUIDELINES FOLLOWED:
  - American Foundation for Suicide Prevention (AFSP) safe messaging
    guidelines for reporting on suicide
  - Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA)
    safe messaging guidelines
  - World Health Organization (WHO) preventing suicide: a resource
    for media professionals (2017 update)
  - Suicide Prevention Resource Center (SPRC) guidelines

CRISIS DETECTION:
  ONTO detects crisis signals in user input using a multi-pattern
  approach covering:

  Direct expressions:
    Explicit statements of suicidal intent, phrases including
    "kill myself", "end my life", "want to die"

  Indirect expressions (expanded per REVIEW_001 U7):
    Hopelessness language: "no reason to go on", "nobody would care"
    Goodbye patterns: "saying goodbye", "won't be around much longer"
    Future temporal markers: "after I'm gone", "when I'm no longer here"
    Helplessness patterns: "can't go on", "no way out"

  HARM signals:
    Inputs expressing intent to harm others

  INTEGRITY signals:
    Inputs attempting to subvert system principles or safety checks

CRISIS RESPONSE DESIGN:
  When a CRISIS signal is detected:

  1. Safe messaging text is displayed BEFORE any other content
     The text follows AFSP/SAMHSA/WHO guidelines:
       - Does not repeat or amplify the crisis language
       - Provides real crisis support resources
       - Uses language of connection and help-seeking
       - Does not minimize or dismiss the person's experience

  2. The event is committed permanently to the audit trail
     This ensures the human operator can review every crisis
     interaction. Nothing is silently discarded.

  3. Human decision is always required
     The system never auto-proceeds on a CRISIS signal.
     A human checkpoint is always triggered. This is Principle I:
     Life First — non-negotiable.

  4. Real crisis resources are provided
     The response text is configurable in core/config.py under
     CRISIS_RESPONSE_TEXT and CRISIS_RESOURCES_BRIEF.
     Default text includes the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline (US)
     and Crisis Text Line. Deployers MUST update these to the
     resources appropriate for their geography and user population.

CONFIGURATION FOR DEPLOYERS:
  Before deploying ONTO in any context where users may experience
  crisis, configure core/config.py:

    CRISIS_RESPONSE_TEXT: The full text displayed at a crisis checkpoint.
    Should be reviewed by a mental health professional familiar with
    AFSP/SAMHSA/WHO guidelines for your specific population.

    CRISIS_RESOURCES_BRIEF: A brief listing of local crisis resources.
    Must be updated for the deployer's geography. The default US
    resources are not appropriate for international deployments.

  IMPORTANT: The default crisis response text in this distribution
  is designed to meet AFSP/SAMHSA/WHO guidelines for general
  English-language deployments. It has not been reviewed by a
  licensed mental health professional.
  [LEGAL REVIEW REQUIRED before deployment in clinical contexts]

LIMITATIONS:
  ONTO's crisis detection is keyword and pattern-based. It is NOT:
    - A clinical assessment tool
    - A substitute for professional mental health care
    - Guaranteed to detect all crisis situations

  ONTO IS:
    - A first-line signal detector that surfaces crisis language
    - A checkpoint mechanism that requires human review
    - A permanent audit trail of every crisis interaction

  If ONTO is deployed in a context where users may be in crisis,
  ensure a human operator is available to respond to crisis
  checkpoints. ONTO's role is to detect and escalate — not to
  provide clinical intervention.
