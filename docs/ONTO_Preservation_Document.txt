A NOTE BEFORE READING

This document exists because good intentions are not enough.
History is full of projects that started with noble purpose
and ended serving the opposite.

This document is the answer to one question:

  "If everything changes — what must never change?"

Read it as a commitment. Not from a company. Not from a council.
From the people who built this — to the people who use it,
depend on it, and trust it.

PART I — WHAT MUST NEVER CHANGE

Three things are non-negotiable. Under any circumstance.
Under any ownership. Under any pressure. Under any law
that does not directly conflict with human safety.

1. THE PRINCIPLES

   The 13 principles are the foundation of this system.
   They cannot be suspended, amended, or quietly ignored.
   They apply to everyone equally — users, contributors,
   owners, acquirers, and governments.

   They are not a policy. They are structural.
   The system enforces them on every boot, before every action.

   If the principles are ever changed by anyone —
   the system refuses to operate.
   The world is notified by the public hash.
   The divergence is documented permanently.

2. THE OPEN SOURCE VERSION

   A free, fully functional version of ONTO must always exist.
   Available to anyone. Without cost. Without account.
   Without internet. Without permission.

   No acquisition, partnership, or commercial arrangement
   can remove this. Ever.

   If the open source version is ever abandoned —
   the community has the right and the means to continue it.
   The MIT license guarantees this.

3. THE COMMUNITY

   The people who use ONTO — individuals, researchers,
   developers, and everyday people — must never be
   exploited by the system they trust.

   Their data belongs to them.
   Their decisions belong to them.
   Their trust is not a product to be sold.

   Any commercial arrangement that treats users as
   the product rather than the purpose is a violation
   of these principles. Full stop.

PART II — HOW IT IS PROTECTED

Three layers of protection. No single point of human failure.
No council. No committee. No politics.

LAYER 1 — TECHNICAL PROTECTION (The Hash)

   The principles are cryptographically sealed.
   Their SHA-256 hash is published publicly and permanently.

   Verified hash:
   b1d3054f646c5f3abaffd3a275683949aef426d135cf823e7b3665fb06a03ba5

   Public record:
   https://gist.github.com/bnyhil31-afk/6436e717a619b9a4d685f5b8709a53c8

   Anyone, anywhere, at any time can verify the principles
   have not been changed. No authority required.
   No permission required. No trust required.

   The code itself performs this verification on every boot.
   If verification fails — the system stops completely.

   This layer requires no human to enforce it.
   It enforces itself.

LAYER 2 — COMMUNITY PROTECTION (Witness and Record)

   The open source community serves as the collective witness.

   Not as an authority. Not as a governing body.
   Simply as people who are paying attention.

   If any version of ONTO — commercial, forked, or modified —
   diverges from the principles, any community member has
   the right and the responsibility to:

   — Document the divergence publicly
   — Reference the original principles and hash
   — Make the divergence part of the permanent record

   No vote required. No approval required.
   Truth does not need permission.

   This layer requires no single person to enforce it.
   It is enforced by many.

LAYER 3 — IDENTITY PROTECTION (The Name)

   The name ONTO and its associated identity belong to
   the original project and its principles.

   Any fork that removes or overrides the principles
   may not present itself as ONTO.

   It may be a fork of ONTO's code.
   It is not ONTO.

   The distinction matters — because users deserve to know
   what they are trusting. A system without the principles
   is a different system. It should say so.

   This layer requires no enforcement by any individual.
   The public record makes the distinction clear.

PART III — IF ONTO IS ACQUIRED

If ONTO is ever acquired by any individual, company,
institution, or government body, the following conditions
are non-negotiable and must be included in any
acquisition agreement:

1. The 13 principles remain intact and enforced.
   The acquiring party cannot modify, suspend, or override them.

2. The open source version remains free and available.
   The acquiring party must maintain it or release it
   to the community to maintain.

3. User data is not used for purposes beyond
   what users have consented to.
   The acquiring party cannot retroactively change
   the terms under which existing data was collected.

4. The public hash record remains published
   and independently accessible.

5. This preservation document becomes part of
   any acquisition agreement — by reference or inclusion.

If any of these conditions cannot be met —
the acquisition should not proceed.

These are not negotiating points.
They are the price of the name.

PART IV — IF ONTO IS FORKED

Anyone may fork ONTO's code. The MIT license guarantees this.
Forks are welcomed. Forks are how open source grows.

However:

A fork that maintains the principles in full
may present itself as built on ONTO.

A fork that removes or overrides the principles
must make this clear to its users.
It may not use the ONTO name without qualification.
The divergence must be documented and public.

This is not a legal threat. It is a clarity requirement.
Users deserve to know what they are trusting.

PART V — IF THE ORIGINAL AUTHORS ARE GONE

This project was built by a person.
People are mortal. People move on. People change.

If the original authors are ever unable or unwilling
to maintain ONTO — the following applies:

1. The open source version passes to the community.
   The MIT license already ensures this.
   Anyone may continue it.

2. The principles travel with the code.
   They cannot be separated from it.
   The hash remains the verification standard.

3. No individual or organization automatically
   inherits authority over the principles.
   The principles govern themselves technically.
   The community witnesses socially.

4. Anyone who continues the project takes on
   the same commitment documented here.
   Not because they are required to.
   Because that is what it means to carry this forward.

PART VI — A PERSONAL NOTE

This system was built in the belief that technology
should serve people — not the other way around.

That belief is not naive. It is a choice.
Made deliberately. Documented publicly.
Enforced structurally.

The people who built this are bound by these commitments
first — before anyone else.

If we ever fall short — say so.
The record will show it.
And we will do better.

That is the only promise we can make with certainty.
And we make it openly, to everyone.
